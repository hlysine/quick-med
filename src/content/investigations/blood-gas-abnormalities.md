# Blood Gas Abnormalities

## First things first - is it an ABG or VBG?
* The lab result may not tell you whether it is ABG or VBG, or may be wrongly labelled; the best way to tell is to **look at the O2 saturation** - if it is low and the patient did not have a corresponding history of desaturation [i.e. check SpO2 at the collection time], then it is a VBG
* If it is a VBG - then only the **metabolic parameters (pH, HCO3, BE)** are meaningful; ignore the respiratory parameters (pO2, pCO2, O2 saturation)
    * However, if VBG pH low (acidosis) while HCO3 and BE are not low (no metabolic acidosis) -> **suspect respiratory alkalosis, take ABG to confirm!!** (especially if pCO2 is very high e.g. >10)
* If it is an ABG, interpret all the values in the clinical context which it was taken - was it taken during desaturation, or at current ventilator/BiPAP settings, or during weaning ventilatory support?

## Respiratory Acidosis
* **Means T2RF (hypoventilation)**, need ventilatory support (non-invasive vs invasive)
* If it is a weaning ABG: put the patient back on the most recent settings before weaning
* If it is taken while on ventilatory support:
    * If it is taken after started NIV for AECOPD: **call MO!**
        * Ddx: mask leak, circuit leak, over-oxygenation (lost hypoxic drive), **inadequate IPAP, insufficient time on ventilator** (max allow 1-2hr)
        * If the above already optimized: **means failed NIV, need prompt intubation!**
    * In other situations, can try increase RR by 2 (up to 20) and IPAP by 2 (up to 20) or TV by 50ml (up to **8ml/kg ideal BW**), then repeat ABG 1-2hr later
* If not on ventilatory support: call MO to discuss intubation or BiPAP with patient and family
    * **Invasive ventilation**
        * Typical initial ventilator settings: SIMV mode; RR 12-16, TV **6-8ml/kg ideal body weight** (~400-500ml), FiO2 titrate down from 1.0 keep SpO2 >92%, PEEP 5; I:E 1:2 (1:3 in obstructive airway, 1:1.5 in poor compliance e.g. ARDS)
        * Typical sedation: Dormicum/ Midazolam +/- morphine (if both, usually called M&M) both 45mg in 45ml NS start at 1ml/hr, W/H if SBP <90; titrate by +/- 1ml/hr
        * Repeat ABG 1hr after initiation to adjust settings
    * **BiPAP for T2RF with respiratory acidosis** (check C/I for NIV!)
        * Typical initial BiPAP settings: IPAP 14 EPAP 4 RR 14 FiO2 0.4
        * (RR is just for backup as patient on BiPAP should be conscious)
        * Repeat ABG 1hr after initiation to adjust settings
    * **CPAP for APO or OSA** (keep airways & alveoli open) (check C/I for NIV!)

> **C/I for NIV (need intubation):**
> * **A:** fixed upper airway obstruction, excessive secretions
> * **B:** frank/impending respiratory arrest, undrained PTX
> * **C:** hypotension, arrhythmia, cardiac arrest
> * **D:** GCS < 8 (unable to protect airway)
> * **E:** facial trauma/burns

> **DO NOT give NaHCO3 for respiratory acidosis!** Correcting the pH may further impair respiratory drive and the HCO3 may be metabolised to even more CO2!

## Respiratory alkalosis
* **Means hyperventilation**
* If on ventilator/BiPAP: can decrease IPAP by 2, or TV by e.g. 50ml, or RR by 2
    * But need to take another ABG 1hr later to ensure no hypoventilation after changing setting
* If not on ventilatory support: advise patient not to hyperventilate so much

> **Pitfall:** in asthma exacerbation, you **EXPECT hypocapnia** (low pCO2) due to strenuous breathing! A **normal pCO2 is a warning sign** of severe attack leading to T2RF!

## Metabolic Acidosis
* **Anion gap (AG) = Na - Cl - HCO3** [normal range: 4-12] or **Na + K - Cl - HCO3** [normal range: 8-16]
* **Causes:**
    * **High anion gap metabolic acidosis (HAGMA): KULT**
        * **Ketoacidosis: diabetic**, alcoholic, starvation
        * **Uraemia**
        * **Lactate: shock, hypoxaemia, seizure, sepsis, organ ischaemia, metformin**
        * **Toxin:** salicylates, methanol, ethylene glycol, paraldehyde, toluene
    * **Normal anion gap metabolic acidosis (NAGMA)**
        * GI HCO3 loss: **diarrhoea, high output drain/stoma**
        * Renal HCO3 loss: type 2 RTA
        * Impaired renal acid retention: type 1, 4 RTA, renal hypoperfusion
* **HAGMA management: order BHBA, urine ketone, lactate, +/- serum and urine toxicology**
    * Ketoacidosis: rehydration (refer to section on H'stix for DKA management)
    * Lactic acidosis:
        * If abd pain +ve: consider CT (angiogram) for ischaemic bowel
        * If on metformin: stop metformin and label adverse drug reaction (ADR) of metformin-associated lactic acidosis (MALA) in CMS
* **NAGMA management: check I/O, use balanced crystalloids e.g. Hartmann/Plasmalyte (not NS)**
    * If not explained by GI loss: workup for RTA (e.g. urine pH, urine AG)

> You can order AG as a test!

> **Indications for NaHCO3 in metabolic acidosis:**
> pH < 7.15, or pH < 7.25 with AKI -> reduces use of RRT

## Metabolic Alkalosis
* **Causes:**
    * Volume (chloride) depletion: vomiting, high R/T output, diuretic, Bartter syndrome
    * Hyperadrenocorticoidism: Cushing's, hyperaldosteronism
    * Severe hypoK
    * Excessive alkali intake
* **Correct volume deficit with NS** (most cases are chloride responsive i.e. saline responsive)
* **Check RFT and correct hypoK**
* If saline unresponsive: order urine Cl, +/- ARR, LDDST

# Hyponatraemia

> **Normal range: 135-145 mmol/L**
> Correct [Na] at a rate around **< 6-8 mM over 24 hrs**
> Usually treat when **< 125 or symptomatic**

## Signs and Symptoms of Hyponatraemia
In real life the patients are **usually asymptomatic** (*much more worrying if they were symptomatic!*)

| Severity | Plasma Na (mmol/L) | S/S | Comment |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Normal** | **135-145** | / | / |
| **Mild** | 130-135 | No S/S, self-limiting | 14% of inpatients |
| **Moderate** | 125-130 | Anorexia, nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain | |
| **Severe** | 115-125 | | 1% of inpatients esp elderly, increased mortality |
| **Dangerous** | **<115** | Life-threatening seizures and coma | |

Clinical significance: Rapid changes of sodium concentration and osmolality causes swelling or shrinkage of brain cells.

> Correct Na **slowly!** Pay attention to **Na trend** when screening lab - difference **>10 mM/day is alarming!**

## Causes of Hyponatraemia
Divided by **VOLUME STATUS**

* **Hypovolaemic**
    * GI loss - vomiting, diarrhoea
    * Non-sensible skin loss
    * **\*Renal loss - diuretics use, mineralocorticoid deficiency**
* **Euvolaemic**
    * **\*SIADH** (*diagnosis of exclusion!*)
    * \*Hypocortisolism
    * \*Hypothyroidism
    * Excess IV fluids / primary polydipsia / beer potomania
* **Hypervolaemic**
    * CHF
    * Cirrhosis
    * Nephrotic syndrome
    * \*Severe renal failure

### Pseudohyponatraemia:
* Dilutional causes e.g. hyperglycaemia
* Hyperglobulinaemia (e.g. multiple myeloma)

> In true (hypo-osmolar) hypoNa, normally body will suppress ADH and kidneys will try to conserve Na, such that urine osmo <100 and urine Na <20
> **\*If urine osmo >100 or urine Na >20 -> kidney problem or increased ADH**

## Assessment
* Most important is to rule out **symptomatic** hyponatraemia (ask for neurological symptoms)
* **Fluid status**
* **Possibility of drip arm** (look at overall results)

## Management
* **If symptomatic:** neuro-obs Q4H +/- CT brain, consult ICU
* **Investigations**
    * Bld x osmolality, TFT, AM cortisol [**especially if euvolaemic**]
    * Paired urine x osmolality, sodium
    * **RFT Q12H to monitor trend, aim raise Na by < 6-8mM/day**
* **Replacement**
    * Dry: NS (IVF, rate depends on how dry you think they are) or 1/2:1/2 if DM patients (basically not D5 is okay)
    * Euvolaemic: **NaCl PO tablet + fluid restriction <1-1.2L (if SIADH)**
        * Dosage is okay depending on Na level, 900mg per tablet
        * Can start with 900mg BD/ TDS x 3/7; step up to 1800mg/ 2700mg as indicated
        * **[common pitfall: if CHF, may be euvolaemic only after diuretics -> check PMH!]**
    * Hypervolaemic: treat underlying cause, e.g. fluid restriction with low salt diet, IV Lasix
        * Lasix 40mg Q8H (20mg Q8H or 20mg Q12H if borderline BP)
        * Aldactone if cirrhosis

> It can be difficult to tell mild hypovolaemia from euvolaemia; urea:Cr ratio may help. If not confident to commit, don't give IVF or fluid restriction; just give NaCl tablet.

> If rate of correction **>10 mM/day:** may cause **osmotic demyelination syndrome!!** Check for neurological symptoms, and **slow or reverse the correction!!**

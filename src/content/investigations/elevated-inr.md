# Elevated INR

## Approach to "abnormal" INR
* Check if patient is **on warfarin**
    * If yes, then **target INR is 2-3** (or 2.5-3.5 if mechanical mitral valve)
    * Sometimes the lab results would have labelled "Yes" on warfarin to help you
    * **If NO, and the INR is grossly deranged (e.g. > 8.00), consider lab error/ haemolysed sample**
        * Make sure the patient is not actively bleeding, urgent recheck clotting profile
        * Usually is fake alarm
* **Liver enzymes**
    * Liver produces proteins involved in blood clotting
    * Liver disease can cause increase in INR
* **Any recent new drugs**
    * Drug-drug interaction with warfarin (e.g. CYP inhibitors)
    * Antibiotics may prolong INR (e.g. Sulperazon)

### Examples of common CYP inhibitors
* Erythromycin
* Ketoconazole
* Cimetidine
* Amiodarone
* Clarithromycin
* Verapamil
* Diltiazem
* Grapefruit juice

## Management
* Assess vitals
* Look for any possible source of bleeding
* Repeat CBC (for Hb drop) and clotting mane, + LFT / mixing study if unknown cause
* **No IMI/ arterial puncture** (e.g. femoral blood taking)
* **Withhold antiplatelets and anticoagulants (including warfarin if beyond INR target)**
    * *Do not stop DAPT for ACS on your own; inform MO if clinical bleeding*
* If **NOT** on warfarin, **no bleeding**: monitor INR, consider PO/IV vit K1
* If **INR > 1.5, NOT on warfarin, active bleeding: transfuse 4 units FFP full rate**
* If **on warfarin**: determine indication for reversal (see table below)

## Reversal of Warfarin

| INR | Bleeding? | Recommendation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **< 4.5** | No | Reduce dose or omit next few doses with daily INR monitoring until INR returns to therapeutic range |
| **4.5-8** | No | If no risk factors for bleeding, omit next few doses (daily INR)<br>If risk factors for bleeding are present, administer low dose (1.0-2.5mg oral vitamin K) |
| **>= 8** | No | **No risk factors for bleeding**<br>- 2013 Australasian Society: 3.0-3.5 mg oral vitamin K<br>- Harrison's: 2.5-5mg oral vitamin K (*but there is no evidence that doing so reduces bleeding risk*) (No recommendations from 2018 ASH guideline)<br>**With risk factors for bleeding** Prothrombin complex concentrate (PCC) 15-30 IU/kg *Note that there is NO PCC in PWH -> give FFP instead?* |
| **>= 2.0** | Yes (significant) | Vitamin K 5-10mg **intravenous** or PCC 35-50 IU/kg IV -> Plasma 15ml/kg if PCC not available |
| **Any** | Life-threatening | - **Give FFP 2 units IV by rapid infusion**, check INR 15 minutes after completion of infusion (if INR >= 1.5, give another 2 units of FFP IV rapid infusion and repeat until INR <= 1.5)<br>- **Vitamin K 10mg intravenous** (over 10-20 minutes)<br>- Withhold warfarin |

* **Avoid using large dose of vitamin K** due to long half-life, difficult to re-titrate warfarin afterwards
* If INR > 4, consider PO 2.5mg vitamin K if active bleeding
* Best if give 1mg by 1mg if IV (according to Vincent Wong) unless really life-threatening bleeding
* PCC is preferred over FFP where rapid reversal of warfarin is needed or when there is risk of fluid overload (e.g. renal failure)
* Beriplex is 4-factor PCC (II, VII, IX, X). If not a/v, 3-factor PCC does not contain factor VII so must give FFP despite its transient effect and risk of volume overload

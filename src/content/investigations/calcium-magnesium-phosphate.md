# Abnormalities in Calcium / Magnesium / Phosphate

## Hypercalcaemia

| Severity        | Normal          | Mild                                        | Moderate  | Severe                                                                           | Dangerous                                      |
|:----------------|:----------------|:--------------------------------------------|:----------|:---------------------------------------------------------------------------------|:-----------------------------------------------|
| **Plasma Ca2+** | **2.10 - 2.55** | 2.55 - 3.0                                  | 3.0 - 3.5 | > 3.5                                                                            | /                                              |
| **Symptoms**    | /               | No symptoms                                 | /         | - Thirst<br>- Polyuria and polydipsia<br>- Nausea and vomiting<br>- Constipation | - Impaired consciousness<br>- Comma<br>- Death |
| **Notes**       | /               | Associated with primary hyperparathyroidism | /         | /                                                                                | Associated with malignancy                     |

* Normal range: **2.10-2.55**
* Adjusted [Ca] = measured [Ca] + 0.02 x (40-[albumin]) -> *but is unreliable; best order ionised Ca*

### Causes
* **HyperPTH (increased Ca, decreased PO4):** primary (adenoma/hyperplasia/CA), tertiary (CKD)
* **Hypercalcaemia of malignancy** (bone erosion, PTHrP, ectopic calcitriol)
* **Drug-induced:** thiazide, vitamin D/calcium supplement, vitamin A
* Less common: TB, milk-alkali syndrome, hyperthyroidism (increased bone turnover), FHH

### Assessment (causes and complications)
* Any symptoms
* Volume status (typically related to **dehydration**)
* Related drug use
* Investigations
    * Bld x **ionised calcium**, PO4, RFT, PTH
    * ECG: **shortening of QT interval, bizarre looking QRS complexes**
    * +/- 24hr urine Ca or fractional excretion of Ca (FHH)

### Management
* **Off thiazide, calcium/vitamin D, vitamin A, antacids**
* **Fluids replacement** (if **hypercalcemic crisis**: NS 200-500ml/hr, aim U/O 100ml/hr if Cr normal)
* Monitor I/O, electrolytes (Na, K, Ca, Mg), RFT and ECG
* **AFTER adequate rehydration: enhance renal excretion of Ca with loop diuretics (e.g. Lasix)**
* +/- IV pamidronate 30-90 mg in 250-500ml NS over 4-6 hours (**bisphosphonate C/I in renal failure!**)
* +/- SC salmon calcitonin 4IU/kg Q12H (only used in ICU for rapid lowering of Ca; onset 2-3hrs)
    * *Note: calcitonin nasal spray is for osteoporosis, not for hypercalcaemia*
* +/- IV hydrocortisone 5mg/kg Q8H (useful in vitamin D intoxication, haematological malignancy)
* +/- consult renal/ICU for HD if severe and symptomatic

## Hypocalcaemia

### Causes
* **CKD** (secondary hyperPTH)
* Hungry bone syndrome (post-parathyroidectomy) -> **need aggressive replacement**
* **Vitamin D deficiency**
* **Hypomagnesaemia**
* Sequestration: pancreatitis, rhabdomyolysis, tumour lysis

> **Remember to adjust for albumin (often low!)**
> Adjusted [Ca] = measured [Ca] + 0.02 x (40-[albumin])
> -> *but is unreliable; best order ionised Ca*

### Assessment (complications)
* Chovstek and Trousseau signs
* Carpopedal spasm / tetany
* Seizure
* ECG: bradyarrhythmia, AV block, prolonged QT

### Management
* Check **ionised Ca**, PO4, Mg, RFT, PTH, vitamin D
* **Correct hypomagnesaemia**
* **If critically low or symptomatic: IV 10% calcium gluconate 20ml over 10-15 minutes (infusion)**
* Can consider adding Ca to IVF
* **Calcium supplementation at 1-2 g elemental Ca daily**
    * Calcichew = 1g calcium carbonate + 800IU vitamin D
    * **Calcium bicarbonate** (40% by weight elemental calcium) is most commonly used
        * Usually 500mg of elemental calcium per chew (also have tablets or capsules)
            * Caltrate (1500mg x 40% = 600mg Ca)
            * Oscal (1250 mg x 40% = 500mg Ca)
        * **Taken with meals** for better absorption and act as phosphate binder
        * Acidic environment is required for effective absorption of calcium carbonate
        * For patients taking acid-blocking therapy, use other formulations
    * Calcium citrate (21% by weight of elemental calcium)
    * Calcium gluconate (9%, ECG monitoring recommended)
    * Calcium chloride (27%, ECG monitoring recommended)
* **Vitamin D analogue** (high dose)
    * **Calcitriol** (Rocaltrol): 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (*activated vit D3*) 0.25 mcg - 1 mcg daily
    * **Alfacalcidol** (One-alpha): 1alpha-hydroxycholecaciferol (*partially activated vit D3*) 0.5 mcg - 3 mcg daily
* Monitor Ca and PO4 level

> Space out Ca supplement from thyroxine to improve thyroxine absorption!

## Hypomagnesaemia

### Causes
* Malnutrition, NPO with B fluid, **refeeding syndrome** (monitor K and PO4 as well!)
* GI loss: vomiting, diarrhoea
* Renal loss
* **Drugs:** diuretics, amphotericin B, aminoglycosides, PPIs

### Assessment (complications)
* Hyperreflexia
* **ECG: Torsades de pointes**
* **HypoK, hypoCa**

### Management
* Monitor **K, Ca, PO4**
* **Review culprit drugs:** B fluid, cyclosporine, diuretics, aminoglycoside, amphotericin B, PPI
* **Mg supplement**
    * IV 4ml 50% MgSO4 (8mmol) in 500ml NS Q8H x 1 day
    * If urgent: IV 4ml 50% MgSO4 (8mmol) in 20ml NS over 15 min
    * If Torsade: IV 1-2g MgSO4 in 10ml D5 bolus
    * PO Mg L-lactate dihydrate 84-168mg BD (slow release - sustained effect)
    * PO Mg trisilicate mixture 10ml TDS (poor oral bioavailability)
    * PO MgSO4 5-10ml TDS (double Mg absorbed compared to same amount Mg trisilicate)
    * Amiloride can reduce urinary Mg loss

## Hyperphosphataemia
* Usually in **end stage renal disease**
* Or iatrogenic e.g. PO4 overreplacement, fleet enema
* **Beware of other causes e.g. tumour lysis syndrome, rhabdomyolysis, haemolysis**
* Check RFT, Ca/PO4, ALP
* Management
    * Low phosphate diet
    * **Phosphate binder with meal** (leave it to MO) e.g. caltrate, sevelamer, Alusorb
    * RRT

## Hypophosphataemia

### Causes
* Malabsorption, **refeeding syndrome**
* Vitamin D deficiency
* Primary hyperparathyroidism
* Hungry bone syndrome (post-parathyroidectomy)
* Renal tubular defects

### Assessment and Management
* Bld x RFT, Mg, Ca/PO4, ALP, PTH +/- vitamin D
* Usually requires no treatment if PO4 > 0.5mmol/L

### Mild hypoPO4 (< 0.5mmol/L)
* Oral replacement
    * Phosphate Sandoz 1 tab BD/ TDS/ QID x 3 days (16 mmol PO4, 20mmol Na)
    * Phosphate sodium 10ml BD/ TDS x 3 days

### PO4 < 0.3 mmol/L with symptoms
* **IV 6ml K diphosphate (K2HPO4) solution in 500ml D5 Q12H infusion**
    * 14.5mmol PO4 + 25mmol K per 10ml K2HPO4
    * **Beware of fluid overload and hyperK!**
* **IV 20ml Na glycerophosphate (Glycophos) solution in 100ml NS Q4H infusion**
    * 1mmol PO4 + 2mmol Na per ml Glycophos

> **Rapid IV replacement may cause symptomatic hypocalcaemia and CaPO4 precipitation**
> If hypoCa + hypoPO4, prescribe vit D supplement (e.g. Calcichew) instead!

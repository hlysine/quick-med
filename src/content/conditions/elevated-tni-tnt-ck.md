# Elevated TnI / TnT / CK

> **TnI High = ACS??**
> **ACS dx: 2 out of 3 - chest pain, elevated cardiac enzymes, serial ECG ST/T changes**

*Note DM/elderly/female patients may have silent MI/atypical presentation. Can also ask for SOB, N/V, diaphoresis*

If moderate-high clinical suspicion of ACS, must perform **serial trending with both TnI and CK +/- LDH**, as they peak and drop at different times
sequence: myoglobin -> troponin -> CKMB -> AST -> LDH (MTCAL)

## Causes of Elevated TnI
* **ACS**, stable angina
* **Chronic heart failure**, myocarditis, cardiomyopathy
* **Arrhythmia** (esp tachycardia)
* **Pulmonary embolism**, respiratory failure
* **Sepsis**
* **Renal failure**

*hsTnI up to 300+ is actually quite commonly caused by the above causes*
*Not worrying of significant infarct if not doubling in serial blood tests and peak < 400*

## Causes of elevated CK
* **ACS**
* **Rhabdomyolysis**
    * Present with muscle pain, AKI, myoglobinuria
    * Treatment: rehydration with IVF
* Myositis
* Renal failure
* Recent exercise

## ECG interpretation

### Unstable angina/ NSTEMI
* Subendocardial ischemia typically gives horizontal/downsloping ST depression in lateral leads (I, II, V4-6) and variable additional leads
    * Upsloping ST depression is non-specific
* ST depression and TWI do NOT indicate the ischemic territory
    * Localised ST depression is usually reciprocal change of STEMI! (see below)
    * Diffuse ST depression + STE in aVR indicates left main/LAD occlusion, indicated for primary PCI (see below)
* If no STE, unsure if ischemic change or not: repeat ECG Q4H for dynamic change

### STEMI
* **ACC/AHA STE criteria: 2 contiguous leads with J point elevation of**
    * Men < 40 years of age: 2.5 mm in V2-V3 and 1 mm in all other leads
    * Men > 40 years of age: 2 mm in V2-V3 and 1 mm in all other leads
    * Women: 1.5 mm in V2-V3 and 1 mm in all other leads
* **STE: STEMI or not?**
    * Look for associated features: acute change in morphology, **hyperacute/inverted T**, pathological Q, **reciprocal change** in opposite leads
    * **If unsure if STEMI or not: repeat ECG Q15min for interval change**
    * Other cause of STE: pericarditis, high takeoff ST, LV aneurysm, LVH, LBBB, Brugada -> but rarely cause new onset STE (except Takotsubo only)
    * Note: LVH & LBBB typically a/w STE discordant to QRS (i.e. STE + deep S in V1-3), so STE is only significant if 1) amplitude > 1/6 of deep S in V1-3, or 2) present in V4-6 (concordant to QRS)

| Time frame | ECG change | Details |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **5-30 mins** | **Hyper-acute T** | - Peaked T-waves<br>- Earliest ECG change in acute coronary syndrome<br>- T-waves taller than QRS complex<br>- Persistence of ischaemia causes peaked T-wave to evolve into ST elevation<br>- **Estimated > 1/2 QRS complex** (*for early anterior MI, there may also be aVR ST elevation*) |
| **Hours** | **ST elevation** | - **>1mm in limb leads, > 2mm in chest leads**<br>- **> 2 consecutive leads for diagnosis**<br>- Age and gender specific |
| **12-24 hours** | **Pathological Q** | - Normal Q waves are found in leads 1, 3, aVF, aVL, V5-6<br>- **> 1mm (0.04s) wide**<br>- Deeper than 25% of total QRS height or >2mm deep<br>- Duration >= 30-40 ms<br>- **Abnormal Q waves often seen in leads V1-3** |
| **Days** | **T-wave inversion** | - Normal in leads 3, aVR, V1, V2 and V3 |
| **2 weeks** | **Resolution** | / |

  II - Inferior        aVL - Lateral        V2 - Septal          V5 - Lateral

  III - Inferior       aVF - Inferior       V3 - Anterior        V6 - Lateral

| Localisation | Arteries involved | Leads involved |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Anterior wall (LV)** | LAD | **V2-V4 [ST elevation]**<br>II, III, aVF (reciprocal leads) [ST depression] |
| **Anterolateral LV** | LAD | **V4-V6 +/- lead I and aVL** |
| **Anteroseptal** | LAD | **V1-V3** |
| **Lateral** | Left circumflex artery | **I, aVL, V5-V6** |
| **Inferior** | Distal RCA | **II, III, aVF** |
| **Posterior** | RCA posterior descending artery | **Lead V1-V4 [tall R waves > 30ms, ST depression]**<br>*Depolarisation of RV is less "shadowed" by the LV forces, and becomes more "obvious and dominant"* |
| **Right ventricle** | Proximal RCA | Right sided ECG: **V3R, V4R** |

* Anterior STEMI (V1-4): reciprocal ST depression in inferior leads
* Lateral STEMI (V5-6, aVL): reciprocal ST depression in inferior leads
    * High lateral MI may have just STE in aVL and ST depression in III!
* Inferior STEMI (II, III, aVF): reciprocal ST depression in lateral leads
    * Look for infarct of other RCA territories:
        * **Posterior STEMI**
            * Horizontal ST depression in V1-3 (UA/NSTEMI typically has ST depression in lateral leads - I, II, V4-6!)
            * Tall, broad R waves (>30ms) in V2-3 (reciprocal change of pathological Q)
            * Upright T (reciprocal change of TWI)
            * Posterior leads (V7-9) pathological Q and STE (not sensitive)
        * **RV MI:** hypotension, R-sided ECG (esp V4R), echo
            * **Give fluids! C/I for IV nitrate, morphine**
        * Bradycardia/heart block (SA nodal artery, AV nodal artery)
    * Ddx: type A aortic dissection affecting ostium of RCA

## Other ECG findings that warrant primary PCI

> **STEMI equivalents:** qualify as "occlusion MI", benefit from primary PCI

* NSTEMI with unstable BP/P, or persistent/recurrent angina despite max medical tx
* New LBBB with unstable BP/P or APO, or satisfy Sgarbossa criteria
* New RBBB+LAFB: proximal LAD
* de Winter ST/T-wave complex (tall symmetrical hyperacute T waves + ST depression >1mm upsloping at J point in precordial leads; reciprocal STE in aVR): usually LAD
* STE in aVR + diffuse ST depression: left main / proximal LAD
* Type A/B Wellens' (biphasic/inverted T waves in V2-3): proximal LAD
    * *Caution: Wellens' pattern occurs during pain-free reperfusion period!*

## Management

### Unstable Angina / Non-Occlusive MI
* Bed rest
* Cardiac monitoring (for arrhythmia)
* SpO2 monitoring, keep O2 > 92%
* BP/P Q4H (for mechanical complications e.g. APO/ shock, rare unless transmural infarct)
* Serial TnI (e.g. Q4-6H) until peak, CBC d/c (rule out type 2 MI), clotting, RFT
    * Serial TnI to assess peak level for estimation of infarct size (for planning PCI later)
    * No need after peaking, downtrend is reassuring
    * If no pain, no need further ECG since already started triple therapy/ not fit for PCI if not fit for triple therapy
    * Recheck if complains of increased pain, if **increased TnI (e.g. > 10000), do ECG for reocclusion**
* Bloods x FG, FL, HbA1c
* **Sublingual TNG 500mcg Q5-15min x 1-3 PRN**
    * Persistent ischemia / HF / HTN: IV isosorbide dinitrate 2-10mg/hr IV (keep SBP>100mmHg)
    * C/I in recent sildenafil intake or RV MI
* **(for MO) DAPT for 12 months + LMWH if not contraindicated** (e.g. active bleeding)
    * Chewable uncoated aspirin 325mg PO stat then 80mg daily with pantoloc coverage
    * Clopidogrel (Plavix) 300mg PO stat then 75 mg daily
    * Enoxaparin (Clexane) 1mg/kg (commonly 40mg) SC Q12H + stat (Q24H if CrCl < 30ml/min)
* **Atorvastatin (Lipitor) 80mg daily + stat**
    * For plaque stabilisation (**regardless of LDL levels**)
* Secondary prevention after stabilisation (rule out acute heart failure)
    * Metoprolol (Betaloc) 25mg BD + stat (contraindicated in RV MI)
    * Losartan 25mg daily + stat (increased to 50mg daily as tolerated)
* Consult AMI nurse x risk stratification (further workup e.g. angiogram, rehab, risk factor modification)

### STEMI / STEMI equivalents
* Same as above PLUS **active primary PCI call**
* Revascularisation **within 12 hours of onset or < 24 hours with ongoing ischaemia**
    * **Primary PCI within 90min, or else thrombolysis (TNK; note C/I!)**
    * Patients C/I for antiplatelet/anticoagulation are also **not** candidates for PCI/thrombolysis (PCI requires continuous heparinisation, and nowadays must include stenting so **DAPT x 12 months** required afterwards)
* Persistent ischemia / APO or acute heart failure / HTN: IV isosorbide dinitrate 2-10mg/hr IV (keep SBP>100mmHg; C/I in sildenafil intake or RV MI)
* **Post PCI**
    * Monitor symptoms, CK (peak 4-8hrs post-reperfusion vs 12hrs without), ECG Q8H x3 (>50% resolution of STE in worse lead at 60-90min post-reperfusion; if not, do rescue PCI! may have accelerated nodal/idioventricular rhythm post-reperfusion)
    * Avoid NSAID/steroid in early recovery as risk of aneurysm and myocardial rupture
    * Monitor wound at radial artery

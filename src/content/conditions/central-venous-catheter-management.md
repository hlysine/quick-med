# Central Venous Catheter Management

## Types of CVC you will see in ward
* **Temporary HD catheters** (without subcutaneous tunnel) - enter jugular vein in *neck* or femoral vein
* **Tunnelled catheters** (e.g. Hickmann) - subcutaneous tunnel in *chest* then join jugular/subclavian vein
* **Peripherically-inserted central cannulas (PICCs)** - enter vein in *arm*, long lumen to reach central vein
* **Port-a-cath** (not a catheter, but a central venous access device)

## Common indications
* Blood taking (e.g. haemat patients)
* Drug administration (e.g. ICU patients: inotropes, concentrated electrolytes, prolonged antibiotics)
* TPN (e.g. surgical patients)
* HD (renal patients)

## Tasks you might need to do
* Confirm CVC position
* Take line blood
* Off central line

> **Review CXR after central line insertion:**
> * **Most important is to look for PTX!**
> * CVC tip should be at the SVC/RA junction
> * For femoral lines, line tip is usually in common femoral vein; it need not reach the IVC

## Technique of blood taking from CVC: good line care prevents line infection/blockage!
* Stop IVF for 3min, TPN for 5min before blood taking (prevent drip arm effect)
* Aseptic technique: on sterile set, prepare **per lumen** - 1 chlorhexidine wipe, 4 10ml syringes (for aerobic sample, anaerobic sample, NS flush, heparin flush), 10ml NS, 5ml heparin (conc. heparin for HD cath; heparinised saline for hemat patients)
* **Rub connecting hub very hard for 15s** with chlorhexidine wipe
* Discard first 5ml of blood if not taking bld c/st; but no need to discard for bld c/st
* After taking blood: flush with 10ml NS using **push-pause-push-pause** technique (create turbulent flow to dislodge clots/platelets/fibrin); then flush with 5ml heparin (conc. heparin for HD cath; heparinised saline for hemat patients), **create positive pressure** (push hard and fast and close the clamp while flushing, to prevent back flow of blood which may clot lumen)
* Remember to **close clamp** (don't leave catheter blood open to air)
* Apply disinfecting cap if a/v (can cap with combi stopper though it doesn't prevent line infection)

> **Tips on taking paired bld c/st (1 set from each CVC lumen + 1 set from peripheral vein)**
> * **Equal volume for all sets:** inoculation volume affects the *time to positive c/st*, which is used to determine likelihood of line infection (**hence whether the line needs to be removed!**)
> * **Clearly label which lumen** the sample was from: abx will be infused into that specific lumen

## Technique for off CVC (jugular or femoral) or arterial sheath
* **Put patient in head down position if CVC at neck; lie flat if CVC/ arterial sheath at femoral vein** (elevate the whole bed so you don't hurt your back since you will need to press it for quite a while!)
* **Remove anchoring stitches** (usually 3 of them)
* **Remove CVC under Valsalva/ end inspiration/ end expiration** (/expiratory phase if cannot cooperate)
* **Remove the CVC in one go and make sure tip is intact, IMMEDIATELY press on exit site** (and more proximal site where the sheath actually enters the blood vessel)
* **Press for 15-20 minutes** to ensure haemostasis (can ask supporting staff/ nursing student to help but recommend do it yourself for arterial sheath)
* **Pressure dressing**
* **Document all steps; obs Q1H x4**

> **CVC at femoral vein v.s. arterial sheath**
> **BE CAREFUL** - blood loss can be rapid or even **spurting** from arterial sheath if manual pressure not properly applied (i.e. **NOT only at exit site, but also more proximally**)
> Bring LOTS of gauze to bedside just in case! Proper pressure dressing afterwards is equally important.

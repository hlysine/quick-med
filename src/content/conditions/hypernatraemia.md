# Hypernatraemia

> **Normal range: 135-145 mmol/L**
> Correct [Na] at a rate around **< 6-8 mM over 24 hrs**

## Causes of Hypernatraemia
More often than not, the patient is **DEHYDRATED**

| | Hypovolemic or Euvolemic | Hypervolemic |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Urine [Na] < 20** | **GI loss of free water** (vomiting, diarrhoea) or **reduced fluid intake i.e. Dehydration** | **MC excess** (Cushing's, Conn's syndrome) |
| **Urine [Na] > 20** | - [urine: plasma osmolality > 1]: Use of diuretics<br>- [urine: plasma osmolality ~ 1]: **Osmotic diuresis**<br>- [urine: plasma osmolality < 1]: **Diabetes insipidus** (large volume of dilute urine, check urine osmolality; < 300 is complete, 300-600 partial DI) | Increase in intake (infusion) |
| **Pathophysiology** | Loss of fluids | Gain of sodium |

## Management
Usually treat if **Na > 150mmol/L**
* If symptomatic: neuro-obs Q4H +/- CT brain, consult ICU
* **Off diuretics and NaCl tablet**, review culprit drugs e.g. lithium (DI), Tazocin or Timentin (high Na load)
* **Rehydration with IVF Q6H or Q8H**
    * **Avoid NS**
    * Use 1/2:1/2 or D5 (prefer not to use D5 if poor H'stix control)
    * Rate determined by fluid status, medical history (e.g. any Hx of CHF)
* **Monitor RFT Q8H or Q12H; aim reduce Na by < 6-8mmol/day**
* +/- Monitor H'stix while on D5
* Underlying cause: check H'stix (DKA/HHS), calcium (hyperCa), serum and urine osmo (DI)

> Correct Na **slowly!** Pay attention to **Na trend** when screening lab - difference **>10 mM/day is alarming!**

> If rate of correction **>10 mM/day:** may cause **cerebral oedema!!** Check for neurological symptoms, and **slow or reverse the correction!!**
